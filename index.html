MEMORIAL

Los Angeles Marks One Year Since Devastating Wildfires

Recovery remains uneven as thousands await permission to rebuild; no residents have returned to Pacific Palisades

▸

California observed a day of remembrance for the January 2025 fires that killed 31 people and destroyed over 16,000 structures across Malibu, Altadena, Pasadena, and Pacific Palisades. Governor Newsom ordered flags at half-staff. One year later, the recovery has been marked by insurance disputes, bureaucratic delays, and difficult decisions. In Pacific Palisades, only one certificate of occupancy has been issued—for a home already under construction before the fire. Survivors describe watching neighbors sell their empty lots while others struggle to determine if they can afford to rebuild.

Key developments
Palisades Fire destroyed 6,837 structures; Eaton Fire in Altadena destroyed 9,414
933 rebuilding permits issued in Pacific Palisades, but no residents have moved back
UCLA report calls for single government authority to coordinate recovery
Suspect arrested in October for allegedly starting the fire that rekindled into the Palisades blaze

▸

SCIENCE

Physicists Narrow Hunt for Universe's Missing 95 Percent

New detectors at near-absolute-zero aim to capture dark matter interactions that may occur once in decades

▸

Scientists at Texas A&M are developing semiconductor detectors equipped with cryogenic quantum sensors to search for dark matter, the invisible substance that along with dark energy comprises roughly 95% of the universe. The detectors operate at temperatures near absolute zero to capture interactions so rare they might occur only once in years or decades. Lead researcher Rupak Mahapatra compares humanity's current understanding to "trying to describe an elephant by only touching its tail." Detection of dark matter would open what physicists call "a new chapter in physics."

Key developments
WIMPs (Weakly Interacting Massive Particles) remain leading dark matter candidate
Same detector technology could aid nuclear non-proliferation monitoring
Research featured in Applied Physics Letters
Detection would help explain what shapes galaxies and drives cosmic expansion
ScienceDaily
